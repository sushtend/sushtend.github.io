Life in 2040

On hype vs misallocation, and what changes when intelligence starts to behave like a utility

Sushrut Tendulkar · 07 Apr 2026 · 8 min read

The conversation that started this

I was having a heated argument with a friend about whether AI is hype or not.

“A lot of money is being poured into data centers,” he said. “A lot of it is going to get wasted.”

“Maybe. But remember the internet bubble?” I said. “Billions were burned laying fiber optic cables under oceans. At the time it looked insane. That cable became the physical backbone of everything we now take for granted.”

“But today’s hype feels way beyond anything we’ve seen before,” he pushed back.

“It does look that way. But think about it — every era has its hype, just amplified by whatever media exists at the time. Imagine if Twitter existed during the dot-com crash, or cable news had covered the Great Depression in real time. The perception of mania scales with the reach of media. The underlying dynamics don’t.”

He nodded, not fully convinced.

“Here’s what I think we’re getting wrong,” I continued. “Hype is not the problem. Misallocation is. Some companies will fail. Some data centers will underperform. That’s a cost of building general infrastructure before the use cases are fully defined — not evidence the thesis is broken.”

“So you’re saying this is an overbuild phase, not a bubble?”

“Exactly. Infrastructure is always overbuilt first. Railways. Electricity. The internet. Usage catches up later, usually in ways nobody predicted. The data centers going up today might look underutilized for five years and fully utilized a decade after that — for use cases we haven’t clearly named yet.”

He paused. “So what actually changes?”

That question stayed with me.

What changes when the cost of intelligence falls

Here’s the cleaner framing: AI isn’t shaping up to be a product. It’s shaping up to be a utility — like electricity. Nobody debates whether electricity is hype. They ask how cheap and reliable it is.

The same will eventually be true of intelligence. When that happens:

  • The cost of thinking falls
  • The cost of creating falls
  • The cost of deciding falls

What stays expensive: time, attention, and trust.

History has a clear pattern when automation lowers costs. In 1930, Keynes predicted “technological unemployment” and a 15-hour work week. He was wrong on the mechanism — we didn’t work less, we shifted to higher cognitive work. Average US working hours dropped from ~60 hours per week in the late 1800s to ~40 today. Not because machines made us lazy, but because they freed capacity for different things.

The same dynamic played out across sectors:

  • Agriculture: less than 2% of the US workforce now farms, versus ~40% a century ago. Output is higher than ever.
  • Manufacturing: robotics made smartphones with thousands of components affordable at global scale.
  • Computing: what once required a room now fits in your pocket. And as Jevons observed, cheaper compute didn’t reduce demand — it exploded it.

Apply the same logic to intelligence. If AI reduces the cost of thinking, analyzing, designing, and coding, services that are currently expensive get repriced. Food, housing, transport, education, healthcare — if these fall 5–10x in real cost over time, the compounding effect on human welfare is significant.

Life in 2040: what structurally changes

Assuming the transition doesn’t stall, here’s what the world looks like on the other side.

1. A personal AI layer becomes default infrastructure

Every individual will have something closer to a “second brain” than a chatbot — a system that knows your goals, your health, your work, your context, and operates continuously.

You stop searching and start stating intent. Less effort on execution, more on direction.

2. Work restructures around outcomes, not roles

The unit of productivity shifts. One person operating AI systems can produce what a small team produces today — already visible in coding, design, content, and analysis.

3. Personal services replace institutions

  • Education: every individual gets a 24/7 adaptive tutor that adjusts to their pace and style.
  • Healthcare: continuous monitoring via wearables plus AI shifts medicine from reactive to predictive.
  • Physical labor at home: “Chores” as a category starts to dissolve.

4. Time becomes the scarce resource

With basic needs cheaper and work restructured, the constraint shifts from survival to purpose. This is a harder problem than it sounds.

As Naval Ravikant frames it: “The purpose of technology is to free humans.” The harder question is what humans do with that freedom. That’s not a technology problem — it’s a human one.

5. Inequality will widen before it narrows

The owners of infrastructure, data, and distribution capture early value asymmetrically. If access is uneven — geographically, economically, by skill level — inequality accelerates before any correction.

6. Human traits reprice upward

When AI handles execution, what humans bring gets more valuable: judgment, taste, trust, relationships, reputation. Identity shifts away from job title toward what you build, what you learn, what you contribute.
